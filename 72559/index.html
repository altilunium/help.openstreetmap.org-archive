Cartographically burnt and recently felled woodland are often indicated on various maps: notably for woodland management, biodiversity, but also for recreation. There are therefore clearly relevant use-cases for this type of thing. In upland areas where forest regeneration is very slow these areas may persist for decades.

As far as I know any tags in use are entirely experimental and have been used by individual mappers at their own discretion. The following are such examples I could find:

  • keys recently_felled & recently_felled;survey_year, see taginfo
  • key forest=felled (e.g., https://www.openstreetmap.org/relation/6844565)
  • key burnt, predominantly used in Russia for a variety of landcovers, see taginfo
  • key wood:lost, see https://taginfo.openstreetmap.org/keys/wood%3Alost. This allows for wind damage as well as burning

Although there is no sign of a single consensus approach to tagging, in many of these cases contributors have recognised how important it is to record the actual date of damage or felling.

I think there are some related points on the Wiki, but I cannot find them at present. Note that in addition to wind, fire, and felling, forests can be damaaged by insect & fungus infestations, avalanches, floods and inundation.
